Automation and Job Displacement in Various Industries

Exploring the economic, security, and ethical implications of automation in the automotive industry and beyond

As automation technologies continue to advance, their impact on employment and various industries becomes increasingly significant. This research project examines how automation is reshaping the automotive industry, focusing on economic effects, security concerns, and ethical considerations. Our goal is to provide a comprehensive analysis that can inform policy decisions and industry practices.

Economic Impact of Automation

Automation is transforming employment patterns and economic dynamics across various sectors, particularly in the automotive industry. Our research reveals both challenges and opportunities as workforces adapt to technological change.

Job Displacement Statistics

Between 1990 and 2007, the increase in robots (about one per thousand workers) reduced the average employment-to-population ratio in a zone by 0.39 percentage points, and average wages by 0.77%. Adding one more robot in a commuting zone reduces employment by six workers in that area.

Source: MIT Sloan Study on Robot Impact

New Job Creation

While robots have displaced unskilled workers on assembly lines, they have also created new jobs for machinists, advanced welders, and other technicians who maintain the machines or use them to perform new tasks. This transformation requires a shift in workforce skills and education.

Source: Brookings Institution Research

Global Perspective

In South Africa's apparel industry, "We find that the impact of automation on unemployment is negligible. In some instances, automation has and is predicted to increase employment by improving productivity." Automation doesn't always have a negative effect on industries and can in fact boost economic activity in certain contexts.

Source: Science Direct Research Paper

Security and Privacy Concerns

As automotive manufacturing becomes increasingly automated and vehicles more connected, new security and privacy challenges emerge that must be addressed.

Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities

Automated assembly lines and robotic systems in manufacturing facilities can be vulnerable to cyberattacks that could halt production or compromise safety. For example, recently CrowdStrike pushed a bad software update that almost shut down the entire airline industry. Similar vulnerabilities exist in automotive manufacturing systems.

Source: Cirium Thought Cloud Analysis

Self-Driving Vehicle Security

Connected and automated vehicles can be targets for hacking, potentially allowing unauthorized access to vehicle systems including steering, braking, and acceleration. This presents significant safety risks as vehicles become more connected and autonomous, requiring robust security protocols.

Data Privacy

Modern vehicles collect vast amounts of data about driving habits, location history, and even biometric information, raising concerns about who owns this data and how it's being used. Insurance companies may buy this data and use it for various means including risk assessment and pricing, potentially without clear consent from vehicle owners.

Ethical and Social Implications

The automation of transportation and manufacturing raises important ethical questions that society must address as these technologies become more prevalent.

Data Ownership

Relating to modern vehicles collecting data, ethical questions arise about who rightfully owns this data and how it is used. Does the owner of the car own the data? Or does the manufacturer/seller of the car? This question has implications for privacy, consumer rights, and business practices.

Autonomous Decision Making

In the aspect of automated cars, ethical concerns come up with what they should do in the event of an accident. The trolley problem is a famous example of this ethical dilemma, where a self-driving car might have to choose between different harmful outcomes, raising questions about how to program machines to make moral decisions.

Source: Brookings Institution Ethics Analysis

Liability Issues

In the event of an accident involving an autonomous vehicle, who would be held responsible? Is it the programmer, manufacturer, or the vehicle owner? One could say that it was the human's fault to get into that situation to begin with, but another could say that the autonomous system should have been programmed better. This creates complex legal and ethical questions that our current systems are not fully prepared to address.

Case Study: Tesla

Tesla represents one of the most prominent examples of automation implementation in the automotive industry, both in manufacturing and self-driving technology.

Factory Automation

Tesla's approach to manufacturing automation has evolved significantly since the company's early days. Initially, CEO Elon Musk pursued an aggressive "lights-out factory" strategy with the Model 3 production, attempting to create a fully automated assembly line. This strategy faced significant challenges, leading Musk to later acknowledge in a tweet on April 13th, 2018 that "Yes, excessive automation at Tesla was a mistake. To be precise, my mistake. Humans are underrated."

The company subsequently rebalanced its approach, finding an optimal mix of human workers and robotic systems. This case demonstrates important lessons about the limitations of automation and the continued importance of human workers even in highly automated environments. Tesla's experience highlights the risks of implementing too much automation too quickly without adequate testing and integration planning.

https://x.com/elonmusk/status/984882630947753984

Full self driving

Tesla's approach to autonomous driving technology differs significantly from competitors. Rather than using lidar technology, Tesla relies primarily on cameras, radar, and ultrasonic sensors combined with machine learning algorithms that improve through data collected from its fleet of vehicles. The company has progressively released increasingly capable versions of its autopilot and full self driving software through over the air updates.

Although these systems have been improving at a rapid rate, there have been many concerns over the saftey of camera versus lidar. Camera's can only recognize and react to data that they are trained on, while lidar can uses lasers and can determine the actual distance, no matter the condition. A recent video by YouTuber Mark Rober demonstrates the inherent advantages of using lidar by putting it through more hypotetical scenarios, and a report by the Wall Street Journal investigated the real world implications and impacts of tesla's choice of a camera based system.
