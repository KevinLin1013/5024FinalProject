Economic Impact of Automation

How automation is reshaping job markets and employment prospects in the automotive sector

[PLACEHOLDER FOR JOB DISPLACEMENT CHART]

Job Displacement Statistics

Between 1990 and 2007, the increase in robots (about one per thousand workers) reduced the average employment-to-population ratio in a zone by 0.39 percentage points, and average wages by 0.77%. Adding one more robot in a commuting zone reduces employment by six workers in that area.

Source: MIT Sloan Study on Robot Impact

[PLACEHOLDER FOR NEW JOBS IMAGE]

New Job Creation

While robots have displaced unskilled workers on assembly lines, they have also created new jobs for machinists, advanced welders, and other technicians who maintain the machines or use them to perform new tasks.

Source: Brookings Institution Research

[PLACEHOLDER FOR GLOBAL COMPARISON CHART]

Global Perspective

In South Africa's apparel industry, "We find that the impact of automation on unemployment is negligible. In some instances, automation has and is predicted to increase employment by improving productivity." Automation doesn't always have a negative effect on industries.

Source: Science Direct Research Paper

Security and Privacy Concerns

The risks associated with increased automation in the automotive industry

[PLACEHOLDER FOR CYBERSECURITY INFOGRAPHIC]

Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities

Automated assembly lines and robotic systems in manufacturing facilities can be vulnerable to cyberattacks that could halt production or compromise safety. For example, recently CrowdStrike pushed a bad software update that almost shut down the entire airline industry.

Source: Cirium Thought Cloud Analysis

Self-Driving Vehicle Security

Connected and automated vehicles can be targets for hacking, potentially allowing unauthorized access to vehicle systems including steering, braking, and acceleration. This presents significant safety risks as vehicles become more connected and autonomous.

[PLACEHOLDER FOR CAR SYSTEMS DIAGRAM]
[PLACEHOLDER FOR DATA COLLECTION VISUALIZATION]

Data Privacy

Modern vehicles collect vast amounts of data about driving habits, location history, and even biometric information, raising concerns about who owns this data and how it's being used. Insurance companies may buy this data and use it for various means including risk assessment and pricing.

Ethical and Social Implications

The moral questions and social consequences of increasing automation

[PLACEHOLDER FOR DATA OWNERSHIP INTERACTIVE]

Data Ownership

Relating to modern vehicles collecting data, ethical questions can come up with who owns this data and how it is used. Does the owner of the car own the data? Or does the manufacturer/seller of the car?

[PLACEHOLDER FOR TROLLEY PROBLEM IMAGE]

Autonomous Decision Making

In the aspect of automated cars, ethical concerns come up with what it should do in the event of an accident. The trolley problem is a famous example of this ethical dilemma.

Source: Brookings Institution Ethics Analysis

[PLACEHOLDER FOR LIABILITY DIAGRAM]

Liability Issues

In the event of an accident, who would be held responsible? Is it the programmer, manufacturer, or the vehicle owner? One could say that it was the human's fault to get into that situation to begin with but another could say that the autonomous system should have been programmed better.
